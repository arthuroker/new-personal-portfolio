As I near graduation, I've been reflecting on my time in college. Specifically, I've thought about my different experiences going through my computer science education compared to my philosophy education. On the whole, I feel like I've enjoyed philosophy more, and in some respects, felt like it's been more instrumental in becoming the thinker and learner I am today. In this reflection, I attempt to understand what explains this difference.

### University Incentives

It's no question that modern universities today act much like corporations. Their product is your educational experience and they seek to increase its perceived value so that they can charge as much as possible. I attempt to state this without making a value judgement, it is not necessarily good or bad. However, I think it begins to explain the difference in experience that I had.

The computer science degree today has seen a surge in enrollment in the past 15 years. The idea of making 6 figures out of college was an allure to make many go into the major. But this is exactly made them want to go into the major. To make the value of the degree match the student's expectations, universities need to structure the degree in such a way that students are industry ready once they come out of university. This has skewed the courses such that it incentivizes hard skills and boosting crystalized intelligence. If I go into a degree program, I should be able come out being able to code in Java.

But of course, universities have not completely pivoted to this bootcamp-like environment, they still need to provide the university experience, so they'll have their usual general education requirements and other mandatory classes that you need to take as well. This, I think, is still of course a good thing, but it feels like two incentive structures pulling at each other, have students come out industry ready and have students come out as holistic thinkers. I think this tensions results in a degraded overall experience.

Whereas I think philosophy does not struggle as much from this. There is no philosophy degree pipeline that students are coming in wanting to be a part of. Many students studying philosophy are either double-majoring in something else, applying for law school, or, rarely, want to pursue further philosophical studies themselves. Because of this, there is less pressure to have students come out with a certain set of facts in their brain, and thus courses can focus much more on teaching students to become better thinkers.

### Professors

This is one of the most obvious differences in education. It has been my experience, that even my "worst" philosophy professor, I've felt was a more effective teacher than my "best" CS professor. This of course is sort of a radical statement and perhaps a bit of an unfair comparison due to the nature of the classes and experience, but I think becomes to make sense as we look into it.

Philosophy professors all have PHDs in philosophy. There is no question. They all were in academia all of their lives. Part of this training is teaching. So on top of becoming radically informed on their area of interest, they have, for years, also had practice teaching this subject. This of course does not guarantee they are any good, but it at least guarantees they have experience.
